Projects

  • "Adiabatic Quantum Control of the Schroedinger Equation", 2016
    Supervisor : Prof. Ugo Boscain, Center of Applied Mathematics (CMAP), Ecole Polytechnique.

    Research in quantum control has shown its impact in various fields such as Nuclear Magnetic Resonance (NMR), photochemistry or even in the design of quantum gates in quantum computing. I was involved in this research while working with M.M.Ouaki and Prof. U.Boscain at the CMAP. I was mainly concerned with the problem of controlling the Schrödinger equation via conical intersections of the eigenvalues of the Hamiltonian. In this project, I focused on the study of a two-level system. I developed a constructive method to control the bilinear Schrödinger equation via two controls, in specific cases, when the system is closed (without a coupling with the environment). In that case I provided accurate analytical solutions to the equation. I found out that one approach could consist in designing a slow control path that passes through eigenvalues intersections in the space of controls allowing exchanges of probability between the intersecting levels, a strategy that takes advantage of the adiabatic theory. In case when the quantum system is coupled with the environment, I had to numerically simulate the behavior of the norm of the wave function and evaluates the dissipation amount for different control paths. Which enabled me to assess the relevancy and effectiveness of each control.

  • "Thermal Dynamic Simulation of a Building Stock", 2015
    Supervisors : Profs.Hassan Bouia & Gilles Plessis , EnerBaT – EDF R&D.

    The goal of the team scientific project was to simulate the thermal behavior of a building stock, so that one could benefit from solar radiated energy and then curb domestic heating costs. One major step was to model the thermal transfer at the interfaces between the inside of the buildings and the environment: an interface could be a wall, a glazing or a thermal bridge, each acts supposedly as a single material layer with specific physical parameters, and each is subject to the heat equation. Afterwards, using the Finite Difference Method, we derived a linear master equation for temperatures of the layers.
    Model of the layers of a wall

    • The state matrix is then reduced by balanced truncation method, specifically, Moore’s method (1981). This work was subsequently complemented with an analysis via a modeling language (BuildSysPro) that replicated the complex system of material layers. To estimate the solar radiated flux, I used a shadow volume technique to measure the sun’s contribution to the heating of buildings. After deriving the locus of the solar shadow for a particular wall on another, I took into account the contribution of all the walls in the stock using an algorithm built and implemented with Python. The final process was time efficient if one considers the right approximation of neglecting the distant buildings.

  • "Clifford Algebras and their applications", 2016
    Supervisor : Prof. Denis Bernard, Department of Physics, Ecole Normale Supérieure.



    The theory of Geometric algebra (a.k.a Clifford algebras) was initiated by W.K. Clifford over 130 years ago. It unifies all branches of physics, and has found rich applications in robotics, signal processing, ray tracing, virtual reality, computer vision, vector field processing, tracking, geographic information systems and neural computing. Clifford Algebras are introduced through a conventional tensor algebra construction (thus called geometric algebra). In this project , I worked with S.Boulebnane. We studied some properties of real Clifford algebras which can be identified as matrix algebras. More generally, the classification of Clifford algebras as complete matrix algebras (or as direct sums of two such algebras) allows us to define and study the pinors and spinors in different dimensions. Hence, We can see that the structure of those pinors/spinors depends more precisely on the dimension and signature of the quadratic space upon which the algebra is built. Finally, we uncovered how interesting Clifford algebras are when it comes to representing general geometric transformations within the general framework of Clifford groups.

  • "Rotating the Bose-Einstein condensate", 2015
    Supervisor : Prof. Romain Poncet

    In order to demonstrate the constraints in a Bose-Einstein Condensate imposed by quantum mechanics, one can try to rotate the condensate by confining it in a rotating ellipsoidal potential. However, the condensate can not rotate in a block like a conventional object. A vortex in the condensate is created at a sufficiently high rotational speed. These are characterized by Points of zero density (i.e. of the points in which the wave function of the condensate vanishes), and around which the fluid is rotating.

    • During this project, we performed a theoretical and numerical study on the dynamics of vortices in Bose-Einstein condensation, using the Gross-Pitaevskii equation in both 1 dimension and 2 dimensions. Subsequently, we provided numerical simulations that reproduced the resluts of the experiences conducted by Jean Dalibard's team at Kastler Brossel Laboratory .

  • "Group Theory : Applications of Polya's Counting Theorem", 2015
    Supervisor : David Hernandez, Department of Mathematics, Ecole Polytechnique.
    Consider the following combinatorics problem : how many essentially different ways are there of colouring the faces of a cube using 3 colors ?
    This question, and many other counting problems that arise from everyday life, can be solved using Polya's powerful counting theorem. This short work revisits Polya's theorem and applies it to solve various counting problems from geometry and ring theory.
